Other Galleries

Currently, there exist many galleries and repositories related to surfaces (or curves) considered in differential geometry. Here, I would like to introduce—albeit without formal permission—some of the websites that I have frequently referred to in my own work.

  • Prof. Sisto Baldo,  Gallery of Minimal Surfaces

    • This site presents an interactive WebGL-based gallery of minimal surfaces, allowing users to explore various geometric features such as smoothness, periodicity, and principal curvature directions. Built with Three.js, it enables real-time manipulation (rotation, zoom, display toggles) of surfaces. A broad range of examples is included, from classical to more advanced surfaces like the Schwarz P-surface and the Gyroid. The focus lies on visual intuition, while theoretical explanations are kept minimal.

  • Prof. Robert Ferréol,  MATHCURVE.COM

    • This website serves as a comprehensive visual and analytic catalog of mathematical surfaces, both classical and modern. Each surface is accompanied by a brief explanation of its defining equations, geometric properties, origin, and historical context. It is especially useful for those seeking a rapid overview of a surface's mathematical background, combining concise theory with illustrative graphics.
    Although interactive 3D models are not provided, explicit parametrizations are frequently available, offering precise mathematical formulations of the surfaces. This site is highly recommended for anyone interested in differential geometry and related fields, from students to active researchers.(Personally, I have learned a great deal from this resource over the years.)

  • Prof. Shoichi Fujimori,  Minimal Surface Gallery

    • This site offers a clear and detailed Japanese-language introduction to minimal surfaces, including their definitions, geometric interpretations, and classical examples such as the helicoid and catenoid. The exposition is supported by numerous diagrams and formulas, making it a valuable resource for students and educators seeking to understand the fundamentals of minimal surface theory in Japanese.

  • Prof. Xah Lee,  3DXM Virtual Math Museum

    • The Virtual Math Museum is a visual archive of mathematical objects, featuring curves, surfaces, and polyhedra in an interactive and pedagogically oriented interface. Educational usability is emphasized, with intuitive controls and explanatory notes accompanying each exhibit. It is particularly suitable as a learning tool for secondary and undergraduate education, and for general audiences interested in mathematical aesthetics.
    In addition, the site includes more advanced content such as minimal surfaces, pseudospherical surfaces, and algebraic surfaces. These entries are accompanied by explanatory notes and scholarly references, making the platform equally valuable for graduate students and researchers.

  • Prof. Xah Lee,  Xah Math

    • This site is a personal compilation of visualizations spanning topics in algebra, geometry, topology, and vector calculus. Though somewhat eclectic in organization, it showcases numerous Java-based applets for interactive exploration (note: many may no longer be supported by modern browsers). The site is notable for its unique visual approach, making it a reference for creative mathematical expression online.

  • Prof. Mario B. Schulz,  Geometric Analysis Gallery

    • This gallery focuses on constant mean curvature (CMC) surfaces, Willmore surfaces, and related geometric structures, with an emphasis on symmetry, periodicity, and visual elegance. The site offers high-quality illustrations based on current research, combining mathematical rigor with aesthetic sensitivity. It is particularly valuable for researchers and graduate students interested in contemporary geometric modeling.

  • Prof. Nich Schmitt,  Geometrie Werkstatt

    • This site provides graphical representations of CMC surfaces in Riemannian space forms, pseudospherical surfaces, and Willmore surfaces, accompanied by references and mathematical context. The visual quality of the graphics is especially striking, supporting both intuitive and formal understanding. Some 3D models are distributed in .jnlp (Java Web Start) format, which may require legacy Java support and are not compatible with modern browsers—care is needed when attempting to view these models.

  • Prof. Nich Schmitt,  The GANG Gallery of Constant Mean Curvature Surfaces

    • This gallery, maintained by the Geometry and Graphics Group (GANG), is a substantial visual and academic archive of constant mean curvature surfaces. Surfaces are organized by category, with accompanying descriptions and high-quality illustrations. It serves as a starting point for both theoretical inquiry and visual reference, particularly suited for specialists and researchers in differential geometry.

  • Prof. Matthias Weber,  Minimal Surface Repository

    • This blog-style site provides ongoing updates and visual content related to minimal surfaces and their generalizations. The entries often feature experimental and artistic renderings, blending creative design with mathematical structure. Rather than a systematic theoretical exposition, it emphasizes aesthetic exploration and visual experimentation, appealing to those interested in the intersection of geometry and art.
